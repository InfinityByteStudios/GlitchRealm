Five Essential Terms for Understanding
Japan's Great Renewal

文明開化

Imperialift

[im-peer-ee-al-lift]
noun
A rapid rise in national power caused by imperial influence and modernization during the Meiji era.
The restoration of Emperor Meiji in 1868 triggered an unprecedented imperialift, propelling Japan from feudal isolation to industrial prominence within a single generation.
1

Nationlock

[nay-shun-lok]
noun
A state in which a country closes itself to outside contact to protect culture and authority, like Japan under the exclusion laws.
For over two centuries, the Tokugawa shogunate maintained a strict nationlock, permitting trade only through the controlled port of Nagasaki and forbidding Japanese citizens from traveling abroad.
2

Oligrachange

[ol-ih-gra-chaynj]
noun
Major reforms directed by a small ruling group, reflecting the Meiji oligarchy transforming government and society.
The oligrachange of the 1870s saw powerful clan leaders abolish the feudal domains, establish a conscript army, and introduce Western legal codes, all decisions made by an elite circle around the imperial throne.
3

Shogunization

[show-gun-ih-zay-shun]
noun
A period of strict control and isolation shaped by military leadership, similar to the shogunate enforcing limited foreign contact.
The era of shogunization ended abruptly when Commodore Perry's black ships arrived in 1853, exposing the vulnerability of Japan's self-imposed seclusion.
4

Treatyshift

[tree-tee-shift]
noun
A forced change in national direction after accepting unequal treaties that reduced independence and opened ports.
The humiliating treatyshift imposed by Western powers, granting them extraterritoriality and limiting Japanese tariffs, became a driving force behind the nation's desperate rush to modernize and renegotiate as equals.
